Biomimetic
SPIDER BOT

BY Aron Michael Delson

Introduction

In the realm of robotics, innovation often takes inspiration from nature's most ingenious designs. One such marvel is the full legged spider robot. Humans cannot travel to every point on the surface, yet they still require information from such locations. As a result, researchers carefully analysed and have designed a cutting-edge technology a biomimetic robot. This portfolio documents my experience in designing and developing a prototype for a four-legged spider. Throughout this module, I have explored various techniques and technologies to create a virtual prototype, which was then translated into a physical prototype.

Product Development Strategy: A Chronological Narrative

Phase 1: Conceptualization (Weeks 1-4)

In the initial phase, our team brainstormed ideas for a conversational AI bot that could be of use the society, something that is also compatible with the university guidelines but at the same time should also look cool enough. We conducted research, put out different idea out there and identified key features that our bot should possess. We then decided to focus on building a four legged spider bot.

Successes:

  • Identified key features and functionalities for the bot
  • Established a clear direction for the project

Challenges:

  • • Difficulty in defining the scope of the project
  • • Limited understanding of the technical requirements

Purpose

A biomimetic robot can explore earth's environment in dangerous and difficult places. By carefully studying the anatomy of a spider, researchers aim was to replicate or mimic the moments of a spider that gives the same range of motion similar to spiders natural movement. This inspired the 3° of freedom fabrication for each leg which minimises the robots weight and complexity. The chosen structure gives the robot manoeuvrability on uneven surface including examining precarious structures like earthquakes, tsunami's or volcanic eruptions.

Phase 2: Virtual Prototyping and Stimulation (Weeks 5-8)

Initially I had to create the virtual robotic exoskeletons. Virtual prototyping is crucial in spider bot making as it enables early software development, reducing the total time necessary to develop and test new products. This makes it cost effective and allows me to carry out a hypothesis-driven virtual experiments on my robot. In this report, we will also talk about the significance of virtual prototyping, the tools used, the challenges faced and the transitioning to physical prototyping. With a clear understanding of the project's scope, the next step was designing the spider bot.

    Motion Capture Hardware

    Motion capture is the process of capturing moments of a sculpture or artist, this moment can then be translated into digital form for precise digital sculpting. This digital model can then be printed into a 3D model to create a physical representation of the original sculpture. This could play a vital role in the development of my spider bot by enabling the precise recording of movement patterns, which can then be replicated or analysed to enhance the bot’s locomotion. I was unable to duplicate anything because I lacked a pre-made model of a spider bot, thus I refrained from using it during the design development process. Other industries benefit from motion capture are:

    • 3D Sports and Fitness Motion capture technology is utilised in the sports and fitness industries to enhance athletic performance. It accomplishes this by giving the user tiny reflective markers to place on common athlete vital locations, such as the knees, shoulders, hips, and joints. These are important starting points for a significant shift that must be documented. The athlete is then captured on camera by a motion capture device while they execute a sequence of actions like running, jumping, and throwing. Their instructors or trainers then examine the captured data to gain important insights about their motions and shortcomings.
    • 3D Video Games and Animation Motion capture technology is used in the video game and animation industries to record people's movements, allowing animators to generate realistic character movements. Wearing a capture suit or a suit with reflective marker points that pre-mark the essential joint movements, an actor executes a series of actions. A motion capture technology is then used to record the actors' motions and gather real-time data. After being converted into a digital format, the captured data is applied to a 3D character model. This 3D character model shows the actor's actions as they would appear in an animated film or video game.

    Design Started the project with the traditional sketching tools like CAD to create rough concepts of the spider bot. The main considerations during this phase were to design the leg of the bot in such a way that the leg could move in multiple degrees of freedom to mimic the movement of a real spier. And at the same time also need a lightweight yet a strong frame to hold the electronics and servos. The 3d modelling software allowed me to sculpt using different drawing tools, shape manipulation and parametric modelling to bring my ideas to life. With these tools, I was able to design a leg to meet the exact specifications required for the bots functionality. Simulation and Testing Once the initial design was complete, simulations were done to test the robot’s movement. 1. Stability check - I had to ensure that the spider bot could maintain balance while walking. The main objective in the initial stage was the placement of the robot leg as it plays a critical role in maintaining the balance of the robot. The robot requires to tilt its torso from left to right, simultaneously the robot should move forward and backward. 2. Range of motion – Had to make sure the legs had sufficient range of motion so that they don’t interfere with each other. Testing and Iteration 3D modelling software often includes animation tool that allows me to test and simulate the movement of spider bots in virtual world. This is particularly useful for testing and refining the bot’s locomotion. The simulation phase revealed several critical insights: • The initial leg design lacked sufficient range of motion, leading to awkward and unstable movement. • The body structure required reinforcement in certain areas to prevent flexing under load. • The placement of the power supply needed adjustment to achieve better balance. These findings led to several iterations in the design, refining the leg joints, reinforcing the body frame, and repositioning components.
